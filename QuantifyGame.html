Quantifiers Guide

Quantifiers are words that precede and modify nouns to indicate quantity. Here's a guide to the most common English quantifiers:

Some / Any

Used with plural countable nouns and uncountable nouns to indicate an unspecified quantity.

I have some books in my bag. (affirmative)
Do you have any sugar? (question)
We don't have any milk left. (negative)

Much / Many

Used to talk about large quantities (much with uncountable nouns, many with countable nouns).

There isn't much time left. (uncountable)
How many people are coming? (countable)
Too much sugar is bad for your health.

A few / A little

Used to talk about small quantities (a few with countable nouns, a little with uncountable nouns).

I have a few friends in London. (countable)
Add a little salt to the soup. (uncountable)
We have a few minutes before the meeting starts.

A lot of / Lots of

Used with both countable and uncountable nouns in affirmative sentences.

There are a lot of apples on the tree. (countable)
She has lots of patience. (uncountable)
We have a lot of work to do.

Few / Little

Used to emphasize that the quantity is smaller than expected or desired.

Few people understand this theory. (countable, negative emphasis)
There's little chance of rain today. (uncountable, negative emphasis)
Few students passed the difficult exam.

Several

Used with plural countable nouns to indicate more than two but not many.

I've been to Paris several times.
There are several options available.
She has several books on the subject.

Enough

Used to indicate that the quantity is sufficient.

We have enough chairs for everyone. (countable)
Is there enough time to finish? (uncountable)
She didn't have enough money to buy it.

All / Most / Some / None

Used to talk about quantities in relation to a whole.

All students must attend the lecture.
Most people enjoy traveling.
Some of the answers were correct.
None of the information was accurate.
